What this shows

This dashboard tracks how far apart news media and social media have drifted in their coverage of Erika Kirk, month by month, since July 2025. It doesn't measure what's true or what's false — only the size of the gap between the two worlds. Each month gets a single composite score: near zero means both channels are talking about the same things in similar ways; a score above 0.60 means they've drifted far enough that they're effectively having two separate conversations about her.

The story is quiet through summer 2025, then explodes in September with the assassination of her husband Charlie Kirk. From there the two channels gradually pull apart through autumn as social media fixates on rumors and controversies that news outlets largely ignore. The widest gap is January–February 2026, when mainstream outlets cover tour stops, courtroom battles, and political endorsements while social media is consumed by pregnancy rumors, an AI-deepfake video, and the Candace Owens "Bride of Charlie" series. The gap then narrows in April after the White House Correspondents' Dinner shooting forces both sides to cover the same real-world event again. Data layer upgraded. Mainstream article counts and tone now come from the GDELT 2.0 DOC API (real article counts, ~25 sample headlines per month, average tone). Sentiment is model-scored by cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment via the HuggingFace Inference Providers API (HF router), run over 220 GDELT headlines and 60 representative social-discussion snippets. Data Sources

Mainstream volume + tone GDELT 2.0 DOC API timelinevol + timelinetone queries for "Erika Kirk", full date range 2025-07-01 → 2026-05-27, daily resolution, aggregated to monthly. Real article-count proxy (GDELT volume intensity) and -100…+100 tone score per day.
Mainstream sentiment (HF) GDELT artlist queries pulled top ~25 articles per month (224 headlines total). Each headline classified by cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment via router.huggingface.co/hf-inference. Per-month sentiment = mean of (p_positive − p_negative).
Social sentiment (HF) Representative social-discussion snippets per month (5–7 per active month, 60 total) grounded in documented viral events from web-search anchors. Same HF model, same scoring. Per-month sentiment = mean of (p_positive − p_negative).
What's still estimated. Social volume remains a derived figure (no Brandwatch / Talkwalker API). Each month's mention count is anchored to a documented viral artifact and tagged HIGH / MED / LOW confidence in the table. Topic clusters per channel are still manually curated from headline + social-snippet analysis (not NLP-extracted).

Framework

Three components, one composite score per month. Components bounded in [0, ~1] so the composite is interpretable as a divergence intensity.

1. Topic overlap (0–1)
Jaccard of dominant topic clusters between mainstream and social.

2. Sentiment delta (-2…+2)
HF-mainstream score − HF-social score, each on [-1, +1].

3. Volume asymmetry (0–1, log-scaled)
(social mentions / GDELT volume intensity) divided by the September 2025 baseline ratio, log-scaled, capped at 1.0.

composite = (1 − topic_overlap) × 0.5 + abs(sentiment_delta) × 0.3 + volume_asymmetry_score × 0.2
volume_score = min(1.0, log10(ratio / Sept_baseline) / 2) where ratio = social_mentions / GDELT_vol

Composite > 0.60 = HIGH DIVERGENCE. Jul/Aug GDELT volume = 0, so volume asymmetry is undefined those months (set to 0).

What Changed From v1 (Estimates → Real Data)

  • July and August are confirmed empty. GDELT shows literal zero mainstream coverage of "Erika Kirk" in both months. v1 guessed ~4 and ~6 articles; the real answer is zero. Erika existed as a name in the public record only after Sept 10, 2025.
  • The four-month "high-divergence plateau" collapses to two months. v1 had Dec '25 – Mar '26 above 0.60; v2 has only Jan and Feb '26 (both exactly at 0.61). Dec and Mar fall just below. The cluster is still there but it's narrower and the absolute heights are lower than v1's worst-case estimates.
  • HF rebalances the sentiment story. Mainstream sentiment from HF is consistently more negative than v1 estimated — driven by GDELT's article mix tilting tabloid (BoredPanda, HollywoodGossip, IBTimes UK, news.meaww.com, economictimes.indiatimes.com). In Nov, Dec and Jan, mainstream is actually more negative than social per HF — the opposite of v1's read.
  • GDELT tone tells a different story than HF. GDELT's tone metric stays mildly negative across all months (−1.4 to −2.9 on the raw scale, ≈ −0.014 to −0.029 normalized) — much milder than HF's per-headline classifier. Two reasons: GDELT tone weights the whole article body (which is more neutral than headlines), while HF only sees headlines (where editors compress negativity).
  • Mar '26 sentiment delta nearly vanishes. v1 estimated +0.65 (huge gap); HF measures +0.013 (effectively zero). The Druski-parody month had mainstream tabloid headlines that were as harsh as the social commentary they were reporting on — the channels converged on tone even though topics diverged.
  • Volume asymmetry shape holds up. Steady climb from Sept '25 baseline, peaking 106× in Mar '26 (Druski) then settling at 25–30× in Apr/May. This is the most robust signal in the dataset.

What the Curve Shape Now Suggests (with hardened data)

  • Big news events compress divergence; quiet months expand it. This v1 finding survives. Sep '25 (assassination, GDELT vol 2.95) and Apr '26 (WHCD shooting, GDELT vol 0.62) are local minima for divergence in their neighborhoods — undeniable events realign the agenda.
  • Topic overlap is the dominant driver of divergence. Months with overlap ≤ 0.20 always score ≥ 0.46; months with overlap ≥ 0.30 never exceed 0.57. Sentiment delta is noisier and frequently flips direction; volume asymmetry adds amplitude but doesn't change the overall ranking.
  • Sentiment delta is unstable. The HF model flips the delta sign five times across the eleven-month series. This is consistent with mainstream coverage of Erika Kirk being structurally heterogeneous (right-leaning sympathetic vs. left-leaning hostile) rather than uniformly opposed to social sentiment.
  • The "Druski month" (Mar '26) is the most volume-asymmetric but not the most divergent. 106× baseline volume gap, but topics overlapped enough (mainstream actually covered the parody) and sentiment was matched. Maximum volume noise ≠ maximum signal divergence — a useful methodological note for the framework.

Caveats and Reproducibility

How to reproduce. GDELT calls are at https://api.gdeltproject.org/api/v2/doc/doc with query=%22Erika+Kirk%22 and mode=timelinevol / timelinetone / artlist. Rate-limit: 1 request per ~5 seconds; the per-month artlist pull required 11 sequential calls with 8–18 s spacing. HF endpoint is https://router.huggingface.co/hf-inference/models/cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment with a token scoped to "Make calls to Inference Providers"; response gives LABEL_0/1/2 probabilities (neg/neu/pos), score = p_pos − p_neg.

Known biases. GDELT's article mix skews global / tabloid / aggregator (BoredPanda, IBTimes UK, Times of India, news.meaww.com appear prominently); a US-mainstream-only signal would require source-list filtering. HF's Twitter-RoBERTa is trained on tweets and may over-classify formal news prose as "neutral." Social-snippet pool (60 strings across 9 active months) is curator-assembled — replacing it with real X/TikTok mention text would change absolute social-sentiment levels without necessarily changing month-to-month shape.

Still soft. Topic clusters are manually curated; social mention volumes are documented-anchored estimates, not API counts. The HIGH/MED/LOW confidence flags on social volume make this explicit per month.

Bottom line: the v2 curve shape is defensible. Two real APIs (GDELT for mainstream volume + tone, HF for model-scored sentiment), 220 real headlines classified, two confirmed-empty baseline months, and a documented-viral-anchor flag on every social-volume figure. The divergence plateau is narrower than v1 indicated and the peak months are Jan/Feb '26 — not the broader Dec–Mar window the estimates suggested.
